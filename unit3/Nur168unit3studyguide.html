🚽 Chapter 38: Urinary Elimination

Master kidney function, urinary terminology, catheters, and diagnostic tests

🫘 Kidneys and Ureters +
📌 Kidney Functions
  • Maintain composition and volume of body fluids
  • Filter and excrete blood constituents not needed; retain those that are needed
  • Excrete waste product (urine)
  • Nephrons maintain and regulate fluid balance through selective reabsorption and secretion
⚠️ Hypervolemia Signs - HIGH YIELD!

A patient with hypervolemia (fluid overload) can present with:

  • Edema
  • High blood pressure
  • Distended neck veins (JVD)
  • Shortness of breath or discomfort
📝 Urinary Terminology - MUST KNOW! 🎯 +
TermDefinition
DysuriaPainful or difficult urination
GlycosuriaSugar (glucose) in the urine

💡 Memory Trick!

AN-uria = NO urine (AN = without)
OLIG-uria = LITTLE urine (Oligo = few/little)
DYS-uria = BAD/painful urination (Dys = difficult)

🔄 Act of Urination (Micturition/Voiding) +
📌 Process of Emptying the Bladder
  1. Detrusor muscle of bladder contracts
  2. Internal sphincter relaxes, urine enters posterior urethra
  3. Muscles of perineum and external sphincter relax
  4. Muscle of abdominal wall contracts slightly
  5. Diaphragm lowers, micturition occurs
💡 Key Anatomy

Detrusor muscle = The smooth muscle of the bladder wall that contracts during urination

🦠 Patients at Risk for UTIs - HIGH YIELD! 🎯 +
⚠️ UTI Risk Factors
  • Sexually active people with female genitalia - female should void AFTER sexual intercourse
  • People who use diaphragms for contraception
  • Postmenopausal people
  • People with indwelling urinary catheter in place
  • People with diabetes mellitus
  • Older adults - decreased bladder contractility leads to retention and stasis

💡 Teaching Point!

Females should void AFTER sexual intercourse to flush bacteria from the urethra and prevent UTIs!

🔧 Types of Urinary Catheters - HIGH YIELD! 🎯 +
TypeDescriptionUses
Intermittent (Straight)Temporary insertion, removed after drainageUrine specimen, acute retention relief, neurogenic bladder self-cath
Indwelling (Foley)Balloon holds catheter in bladder, drains continuouslyICU monitoring, post-op, severe incontinence, end-of-life comfort
SuprapubicSurgically placed through abdomen into bladderUrethral trauma/stricture, long-term drainage, spinal cord injury
🔶 Catheter Insertion Tips!
  • Select appropriate size for patient population (peds vs adult) - larger catheters can cause urethral injury
  • Use STERILE technique for Foley insertion
  • If catheter becomes contaminated, throw it out and start with sterile one
  • Female tip: If you think catheter was placed in vaginal opening, LEAVE it there and insert NEW sterile catheter
  • Always see urine flow BEFORE balloon inflation
  • If patient has pain during balloon inflation → DEFLATE, advance catheter further
🧪 Lab/Diagnostics - HIGH YIELD! 🎯 +
⚠️ 24-Hour Urine Collection

Key instruction: Patient must empty bladder completely upon awakening and DISCARD this urine. This is the START date and time. Then collect ALL urine for 24 hours.

TestWhat It Measures
Urine pHAcidity/alkalinity - abnormal can indicate kidney stones, UTI, metabolic disorders
Specific GravityHow well kidneys balance water and remove waste - identifies dehydration, kidney problems, diabetes insipidus
CreatinineAssesses KIDNEY FUNCTION
💡 Specimen Collection

Urine specimen from patient with indwelling catheter should be obtained from the CATHETER ITSELF, NOT the collection bag!

🗺️ Urinary System Concept Map - COMPLETE OVERVIEW +
🟣 1. ANATOMY - Urinary System Components

Main Structures

  • Kidneys (2) - Located retroperitoneal
  • Ureters (2) - Carry urine to bladder
  • Bladder - Stores urine
  • Urethra - Exits body

Kidney Detail: Nephron

  • Functional unit of the kidney
  • Glomerulus → filters blood
  • Tubules → reabsorption & secretion
🔵 2. PHYSIOLOGY - Urine Formation (3 Steps)
StepLocationWhat Happens
1. FiltrationGlomerulusBlood → Filtrate (water, waste, small molecules pass through)
2. ReabsorptionTubulesWater, glucose, electrolytes RETURN to blood (good stuff saved)
3. SecretionTubulesWaste products ADDED to filtrate (bad stuff dumped)
🟢 3. FUNCTIONS of Urinary System

Waste Removal

  • Removes urea
  • Removes creatinine

Regulation

  • Fluid balance
  • Electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺)
  • Acid-base balance (pH)

Hormone Production

  • Erythropoietin (RBC production)
  • Renin (BP control)
🟡 4. URINATION (Micturition)

Controlled by: Brain + spinal cord, voluntary & involuntary muscles

⭐ NORMAL FINDINGS - MUST KNOW!

Minimum Output: ≥30 mL/hr (less = notify provider!)
Daily Output: ~1200–1500 mL

🔴 5. ASSESSMENT

Subjective (Patient Reports)

  • Pain (dysuria)
  • Frequency, urgency
  • Incontinence

Objective (Nurse Observes)

  • Urine color, clarity, odor
  • Output measurement
  • Bladder distention
🟠 6. COMMON PROBLEMS
ProblemDescriptionCauses/Signs
🚨 Urinary RetentionUnable to empty bladderAnesthesia, obstruction, distended bladder
🚨 Stress IncontinenceLeakage with ↑ pressureLaughing, coughing, sneezing
🚨 Urge IncontinenceSudden urge to voidOveractive bladder
🚨 Overflow IncontinenceBladder overfillsContinuous dribbling
🚨 Functional IncontinenceCan't reach toiletMobility/cognitive issues
🚨 UTIUrinary tract infectionBurning, frequency, cloudy urine
🟤 7. NURSING INTERVENTIONS
  • Monitor I&O (Intake & Output)
  • Encourage fluids (if not contraindicated)
  • Maintain hygiene (wipe front to back)
  • Catheter care (prevent infection, maintain closed system)
  • Bladder training (for incontinence)
⚫ 8. DIAGNOSTICS

BUN & CreatinineEvaluates KIDNEY FUNCTION

💡 Test Question Pattern

Q: Providing a soft, textured blanket stimulates which sense?

A: Stereognosis - perceives size, shape, and TEXTURE of objects

⚠️ Factors Contributing to Sensory Alteration - HIGH YIELD! 🎯 +
AlterationDefinitionAt-Risk Patients
Sensory OverloadTOO MUCH sensory input for brain to processICU patients, surgery, unfamiliar environments, chronic pain
Sensory DeprivationTOO LITTLE stimulationIsolation, visually/hearing impaired, cannot interpret stimuli
Sensory DeficitsImpairments in SPECIFIC sensesOccupational risk (factory workers, airline workers - hearing)
Sensory PovertyDecreased sensory experienceOverreliance on technology
⚠️ Know the Difference!

Overload = TOO MUCH (ICU, monitors, alarms, bright lights)
Deprivation = TOO LITTLE (isolation room, sensory impairment)

💡 Test Question Pattern!

Q: A patient with AIDS in isolation is at risk for which sensory alteration?

A: Sensory DEPRIVATION (decreased/monotonous stimuli)

Q: An ICU patient with multiple monitors is at risk for?

A: Sensory OVERLOAD (excessive stimuli)

🩺 Caring for Visually/Hearing Impaired Patients - HIGH YIELD! 🎯 +
🔶 #1 PRIORITY = SAFETY!

The highest priority when caring for sensory-impaired patients is SAFETY!

👁️ Visually Impaired

  • Acknowledge your presence in the room
  • Speak in normal tone of voice
  • Explain BEFORE touching
  • Keep call light within reach
  • Orient to sounds and furniture arrangement
  • Clear pathways
  • Assist with ambulation
  • Indicate when leaving the room

👂 Hearing Impaired

  • Orient to your presence BEFORE speaking
  • Decrease background noise
  • Check hearing aids
  • Position so light is on YOUR face
  • Face patient directly when talking
  • Use pantomime or sign language
  • Don't chew gum or cover mouth
  • Write ideas if needed
⚠️ EXACT Test Language!

Q: Which measure is appropriate when caring for a hearing-impaired patient?

A: Position yourself so light is on YOUR face - allows patient to see your lips and expressions!

🧠 Communicating with Confused/Unconscious Patients +

😵 Confused Patients

  • Use frequent face-to-face contact
  • Speak calmly, simply, directly
  • Orient and reorient to environment
  • Orient to time, place, person
  • Reinforce reality if delusional
  • Emphasize strengths over weaknesses

😴 Unconscious Patients

  • HEARING is the LAST sense lost!
  • Be careful what you say in patient's presence
  • ASSUME patient CAN hear you
  • Speak in NORMAL tone (not loud)
  • Speak BEFORE touching
  • Keep environmental noise LOW

💡 Critical Point!

HEARING is the LAST sense to be lost! Always assume unconscious patients can hear you. Speak in a NORMAL tone (not loud), and explain before touching.

😰 Chapter 43: Stress and Adaptation

Master stress responses, GAS, LAS, and coping mechanisms

📝 Stress and Adaptation Terms +
TermDefinition
StressA response to changes in the normal balanced state
Physiologic StressorsOriginate from PHYSICAL functioning of the body (chemicals, infection, hypoxia)
Psychosocial StressorsOriginate in relationships, responsibilities, interactions with society
AdaptationBody's ability to adjust and manage stressors to maintain homeostasis
AnxietyResponse to unknown or perceived threat - fear, apprehension, tension
🧬 LAS and GAS - SUPER HIGH YIELD! 🎯🎯🎯 +

🎯 Local Adaptation Syndrome (LAS)

Involves ONLY ONE specific body part

  • Reflex pain response - touch something hot, snatch hand back
  • Inflammatory response

Short-term, localized, focused on immediate protection

🌐 General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

Biochemical model - WHOLE BODY response

Selye's 3 stages:

🧠 GAS - Three Stages (Selye)

A
Alarm
Alarm Reaction
Fight-or-flight response activated. Hormones rise, body prepares to react.
Heart rate ↑, BP ↑, pupils dilate
R
Resistance
Stage of Resistance
Body attempts to ADAPT. Vital signs and hormones stabilize. Body tries to restore balance.
If coping succeeds → homeostasis restored
E
Exhaustion
Stage of Exhaustion
Energy reserves DEPLETED. Defenses break down. NO defense against distress.
Fatigue, illness, depression, immune dysfunction, collapse

"Alarm → Resistance → Exhaustion"

⚠️ Know the Difference!

LAS = LOCAL, one body part (touching hot stove, inflammation)
GAS = GENERAL, whole body biochemical response (Alarm → Resistance → Exhaustion)

📊 Sources of Stress +
TypeDefinitionExamples
Developmental StressOccurs as person progresses through stages of growthTeens forming identity, adults balancing careers, older adults coping with loss
Situational StressDoes NOT occur in predictable patternsIllness, injury, marriage, divorce, loss, new job, role change
📌 Ineffective Individual Coping

Inability to manage or respond effectively to stressors, leading to emotional distress. Often arises from fear of chronic illness, life transitions, or lack of social support.

🧘 Stress Management Techniques - HIGH YIELD! 🎯 +
TechniqueDescription
RelaxationDecreases muscle tension and lowers heart rate
MindfulnessEncourages PRESENT-MOMENT awareness
Anticipatory GuidancePREPARES individuals for upcoming stressors by providing information and expectations
Guided ImageryUses VISUALIZATION to calm the mind - creates mental image, concentrates on it
Crisis InterventionShort-term, structured approach for ACUTE situations
⚠️ EXACT Test Language!

Q: A person creates a mental image, concentrates on it, and becomes less responsive to stimuli. This describes which technique?

A: Guided Imagery (NOT anticipatory guidance!)

🌈 Kübler-Ross's 5 Stages of Grief - SUPER HIGH YIELD! 🎯🎯🎯 +

🧠 DABDA - Five Stages of Grief

D
Denial
Denial & Isolation
"This can't be happening." Refusal to accept reality.
Patient says "The test must be wrong"
A
Anger
Anger
"Why me?" Anger directed at caregivers, family, or fate.
Patient lashes out at nurse or family
B
Bargaining
Bargaining
"If only..." or "Maybe if we do this..." Negotiating for more time.
"If I can just live to see my daughter's wedding..."
D
Depression
Depression
Sadness, withdrawal, hopelessness. Deep mourning.
Patient becomes quiet, withdrawn
A
Acceptance
Acceptance
NOT happiness, but PEACE and acknowledgment. Coming to terms.
"I'm ready. I've had a good life."
⚠️ Critical Points!
  • Stages are NOT linear - they can overlap
  • Stages are individualized - people may cycle through multiple stages
  • People may remain in one stage longer than another
  • Grief varies from person to person
  • Nurses support each stage with nonjudgmental presence
💀 Clinical Signs of Impending Death - HIGH YIELD! 🎯 +
⚠️ Signs Death is Near
  • Difficulty talking or swallowing
  • Nausea, flatus, abdominal distention
  • Urinary and/or bowel incontinence or constipation
  • Loss of movement, sensation, and reflexes
  • Decreasing body temperature, cold or clammy skin
  • Weak, slow, or irregular pulse
  • Decreasing blood pressure
  • Noisy, irregular, or Cheyne-Stokes respirations
  • Restlessness and/or agitation
  • Cooling, mottling, and cyanosis of extremities

💡 Cheyne-Stokes Respirations

Pattern of breathing with gradual increase, then decrease in depth, followed by a period of apnea (no breathing). Common near death.

🚽 URINARY - The Body's Sewage System

🏠 THE BIG PICTURE

Think of your body like a house:

  • Kidneys = The water filter under your sink (cleans the water/blood)
  • Ureters = The pipes carrying filtered water to storage
  • Bladder = The holding tank (like a water balloon)
  • Urethra = The drain pipe to outside
🔤 PEE WORDS - SUPER SIMPLE
WordEasy Way to Remember
Anuria🚫 A = ABSENT = NO pee at all (toilet stays dry)
Oliguria📉 O = Only a little = Just a tiny bit of pee
Dysuria😣 Dys = Difficult = Hurts to pee (ouch!)
Pyuria🦠 Py = Pus = Yucky infection stuff in pee
Hematuria🩸 Hema = Blood = Red blood in pee
Glycosuria🍬 Glyco = Sugar = Sweet stuff in pee (diabetes)
Nocturia🌙 Noct = Night = Waking up to pee at night

⭐ THE 4 GOLDEN RULES OF CATHETERS

1️⃣ Oops, wrong hole? → LEAVE IT, get a NEW clean one
2️⃣ See pee BEFORE you blow up the balloon
3️⃣ Bag goes DOWN (below the bladder) - gravity is your friend!
4️⃣ Get pee samples from the TUBE, not the bag (bag pee is old and gross)

⭐ CAN'T SEE PATIENT - DO THESE THINGS:

1️⃣ "Hey, it's Nurse Des!" (Say who you are)
2️⃣ "I'm going to touch your arm now" (Warn before touching)
3️⃣ Move stuff out of the way (Clear paths = no tripping)
4️⃣ "I'm leaving now!" (Tell them when you go)

⭐ CAN'T HEAR PATIENT - DO THESE THINGS:

1️⃣ Stand so LIGHT is on YOUR face (They need to see your lips!)
2️⃣ Turn off the TV first (Kill background noise)
3️⃣ Face them directly (No talking to the wall)
4️⃣ Don't cover your mouth (Lip reading needs visible lips!)

🚨 THE #1 RULE YOU MUST REMEMBER

"HEARING is the LAST to GO!" 👂
Even if someone looks unconscious - ASSUME THEY CAN HEAR YOU!
Talk normally. Don't say anything rude. They're listening!

☠️ SIGNS SOMEONE IS DYING - THE SAD BUT IMPORTANT LIST

Think of the body slowly shutting down like a computer turning off:

  • Breathing gets weird - Cheyne-Stokes = breaths get big, then small, then STOP, then start again
  • Skin changes - Gets cold, clammy, and MOTTLED (blotchy purple patches)
  • Heart slows down - Weak, slow pulse; blood pressure drops
  • Body stops working - Can't swallow, may lose bladder/bowel control

⭐ GRIEF vs MOURNING - 2-Second Answer

GRIEF = How you FEEL inside (sad, angry, hurt) 💔
MOURNING = What you DO outside (funeral, wearing black, crying publicly) 🖤
