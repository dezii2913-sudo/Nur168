💬 Chapter 8: Communication

Master the art of therapeutic communication in nursing practice

🔄 The Communication Process (Berlo's Model) +
📌 Remember This
Communication is a dynamic, circular process - not just one-way!

🧠 MNEMONIC: "SMCRF" - Communication Process (Hover for details!)

S
Stimulus
Stimulus
What initiates or triggers the need for communication. Something happens that requires a message to be sent.
Patient's blood pressure is elevated → stimulus for nurse to communicate with physician.
M
Message
Message
The actual content being communicated - what is being said or conveyed. Includes verbal and nonverbal elements.
"Your blood pressure is 160/100, which is higher than normal. We need to discuss treatment options."
C
Channel
Channel
The pathway or medium through which the message travels. Can be auditory (hearing), visual (seeing), or kinesthetic (touch).
Face-to-face conversation (auditory + visual), written discharge instructions (visual), demonstration of injection (kinesthetic).
R
Receiver
Receiver/Decoder
The person who receives and interprets the message. They decode the message based on their own understanding and experience.
Patient hears the nurse's explanation and tries to understand what high blood pressure means for them.
F
Feedback
Feedback
The receiver's response that confirms whether the message was understood correctly. Completes the communication loop.
Patient asks: "So I need to take medication and reduce salt to lower my blood pressure?" - confirms understanding.
  • Stimulus: Initiates the communication
  • Sender/Encoder: Person who begins the message
  • Message: The communication product
  • Channel: Auditory, visual, or kinesthetic pathway
  • Receiver/Decoder: Translates and interprets the message
  • Feedback: Confirms the message was received
💡 Clinical Example

Scenario: A nurse needs to teach a patient about their new medication.

  • Stimulus: Patient received a new prescription
  • Sender: The nurse explaining the medication
  • Message: "Take this pill once daily with food"
  • Channel: Verbal (auditory) + written handout (visual)
  • Receiver: The patient listening and reading
  • Feedback: Patient says, "So I take it with breakfast each morning?"
🗣️ Forms of Communication +

💬 Verbal Communication

Depends on understanding of language between sender and receiver

"I'm going to listen to your heart now" or written discharge instructions

🤫 Nonverbal Communication

  • Facial expressions & eye contact
  • Gestures & touch
  • Body movements, posture, gait
  • Space & territoriality
  • Physical appearance & dress
  • Sounds (moaning, crying, sighing)
💡 Nonverbal Examples in Practice
  • Facial expression: Patient grimacing = possible pain, even if they say "I'm fine"
  • Eye contact: Avoiding eye contact may indicate fear, shame, or cultural norm
  • Touch: Holding a patient's hand to provide comfort during bad news
  • Posture: Slumped shoulders and slow gait may indicate depression
  • Sounds: Sighing repeatedly may indicate frustration or anxiety
  • Appearance: Unkempt appearance in usually well-groomed patient = red flag
⚠️ Test Alert
Touch is a personal behavior that means different things to different people - it's NOT universal!
Example: A comforting hand on the shoulder may be welcome to one patient but feel invasive to another, especially across cultures.
📊 Four Levels of Communication +
Level Description Clinical Example
Intrapersonal Self-talk; communication within a person "Okay, I need to stay calm and focused during this code"
Interpersonal Between two or more people to exchange messages Nurse explaining medications to a patient one-on-one
Small Group Communication within a small team Nursing team huddle at shift change; care conference
Organizational Communication across an organization Hospital-wide policy memo; CEO announcement to all staff

💡 Quick Tip

Intrapersonal = Inside yourself (think: intra-venous goes INSIDE the vein)

Interpersonal = Between people (think: inter-state highway goes BETWEEN states)

💡 More Examples
  • Intrapersonal: A nurse reassuring herself before speaking to a difficult family member
  • Interpersonal: Therapeutic conversation between nurse and grieving patient
  • Small Group: Interdisciplinary rounds with MD, RN, PT, and social worker
  • Organizational: New hand hygiene protocol announced hospital-wide
🌡️ Factors Influencing Communication +
📌 Hover over each letter to see examples!
These factors can enhance OR hinder effective communication between nurse and patient.

🧠 MNEMONIC: "DEVS RPM" - Factors Affecting Communication

D
Developmental Level
Developmental Level
Age and cognitive development affect how a person understands and processes information. Communication must be adapted accordingly.
Speaking slowly and using simple words with a child vs. using medical terminology with an adult healthcare professional.
E
Environment
Environment
Physical surroundings can promote or hinder communication. Noise, lighting, privacy, and comfort all play a role.
A patient may not discuss sensitive health issues in a shared room with the curtain open, but will open up in a private space.
V
Values
Values
Personal beliefs and what individuals consider important influence how messages are sent and received. Values shape perspectives on health and illness.
A patient who values autonomy may resist advice, while one who values family input may want relatives involved in all decisions.
S
Sociocultural
Sociocultural Differences
Culture, ethnicity, language, and social background influence communication styles, including eye contact, touch, personal space, and health beliefs.
In some cultures, direct eye contact is disrespectful. Some patients may nod "yes" out of politeness even when they don't understand.
R
Roles & Responsibilities
Roles & Responsibilities
The role a person occupies (nurse, patient, family member, supervisor) affects communication dynamics, power balance, and expectations.
A nurse may communicate differently with a physician (colleague role) vs. a patient (caregiver role) vs. a student (educator role).
P
Physical State
Physical State
Pain, fatigue, illness, hunger, sensory impairments (hearing/vision loss), and medication effects can significantly impact ability to communicate.
A patient in severe pain (8/10) cannot focus on discharge teaching. A patient with hearing loss needs face-to-face communication and may lip-read.
M
Mental/Emotional State
Mental & Emotional State
Anxiety, fear, depression, anger, grief, and cognitive impairments affect how messages are processed and expressed.
An anxious pre-op patient may not retain information. A grieving family member may become angry when receiving bad news. A confused patient may be unable to follow instructions.
T
Territory/Space
Space & Territoriality
Personal space needs vary by individual and culture. Intimate, personal, social, and public zones affect comfort during communication.
Standing too close may make a patient uncomfortable. Sitting at eye level feels less intimidating than standing over a patient in bed.
✅ Nursing Implications
  • Assess each patient's unique communication needs
  • Adapt your communication style accordingly
  • Address barriers before important teaching or conversations
  • Ask if the patient understood (use teach-back method)
📋 SBAR Communication Technique +
📌 Definition
SBAR is a standardized hand-off communication technique used to relay patient information clearly and concisely.

S - Situation

What is happening with the patient RIGHT NOW?

B - Background

What is the clinical background/context?

A - Assessment

What do I think the problem is?

R - Recommendation

What do I think should be done?

💡 SBAR Example: Calling the Physician

Patient: Mrs. Smith, 68 y/o, post-op day 2 hip replacement

  • S - Situation: "I'm calling about Mrs. Smith in room 412. She's developed new onset confusion and her oxygen sat dropped to 88%."
  • B - Background: "She's post-op day 2 from a right hip replacement. She was alert and oriented this morning. History of DVT 5 years ago. Current vitals: BP 142/90, HR 108, RR 24."
  • A - Assessment: "I'm concerned she may have a pulmonary embolism given her recent surgery, history of DVT, and sudden respiratory changes."
  • R - Recommendation: "I think she needs to be evaluated urgently. Can you come assess her? Should I order a stat D-dimer and prepare for a CT angiogram?"
✅ Patient Safety Goal
SBAR supports Joint Commission National Patient Safety Goals for improving communication!
🤝 Therapeutic Nurse-Patient Relationship +
📌 Key Characteristics
  • Does NOT occur spontaneously - requires intention
  • Characterized by unequal sharing (patient shares more)
  • Built on the PATIENT'S needs
  • Nurse is professionally accountable
  • Purposeful and TIME-LIMITED

Three Phases of the Therapeutic Relationship:

Phase What Happens Clinical Example
1. Orientation Establishing the relationship; contract made about goals, frequency, duration "Hi Mr. Jones, I'm Sarah, your nurse today. I'll be caring for you until 7pm. Let's talk about your goals for today."
2. Working Achieving goals; nurse provides assistance, counseling, teaching Teaching patient how to self-administer insulin; working through anxiety about diagnosis; making progress on discharge goals
3. Termination Ending the relationship; examine goals for attainment "Mr. Jones, you've done great learning to manage your diabetes. You're being discharged tomorrow. Let's review everything you've learned."
💡 Example Scenario Through All Phases

Patient: Mrs. Adams, newly diagnosed with heart failure

  • Orientation: Nurse introduces self, explains role, assesses patient's understanding of diagnosis, establishes trust
  • Working: Teaches low-sodium diet, daily weights, medication management; patient asks questions; nurse provides emotional support
  • Termination: Reviews all teaching, confirms patient can demonstrate skills, provides written materials, arranges follow-up care

❓ Interviewing Techniques +
Technique Purpose Example
Open-ended Allows patient to express freely; cannot be answered yes/no "Tell me about your pain" or "How are you feeling about the surgery?"
Closed Specific, limited response expected "Do you have any allergies?" or "Is your pain sharp or dull?"
Validating Confirms what nurse heard or observed "So you're saying the pain started after eating? Did I understand correctly?"
Clarifying Makes unclear statements more understandable "When you say you feel 'funny,' can you describe what that feels like?"
Reflective Encourages deeper exploration "You mentioned feeling scared. Tell me more about that fear."
Sequencing Establishes order of events "What happened first? And then what did you notice next?"
Directing Focuses conversation on specific topic "Let's talk more about the dizziness you mentioned earlier."
⚠️ Test Tip
Validating questions confirm what nurse heard - NOT open-ended questions!
Example: "I want to make sure I understand - you're saying the numbness in your left arm started about an hour ago?" (This VALIDATES understanding)
💪 Assertive vs Aggressive Behavior +

✅ Assertive (GOOD)

  • Confident, open body posture
  • Clear, concise "I" statements
  • Shares thoughts effectively
  • Works with or without supervision
  • Remains calm under supervision
  • Asks for help when needed
  • Admits mistakes, takes responsibility

❌ Aggressive (BAD)

  • Violates rights of others
  • Can be verbal or physical
  • Marked by tension and anger
  • Angry tone, condescending
  • Threatening behavior
  • Focus on "winning at all costs"
  • Inhibits collaboration
🚫 Blocks to Communication +
⚠️ Avoid These!
Block Example of What NOT to Say
Failure to perceive patient as human Referring to patient as "the gallbladder in room 4"
Failure to listen Typing on computer while patient talks; interrupting
Using clichés "Everything happens for a reason" or "Time heals all wounds"
Using "why" questions "Why didn't you take your medication?" (sounds accusatory)
Giving advice "If I were you, I would definitely have the surgery"
Making judgmental comments "You really should have quit smoking years ago"
Changing the subject Patient: "I'm scared about dying." Nurse: "Let's check your blood pressure!"
False reassurance "Don't worry, I'm sure everything will be fine!"
Using gossip Discussing other patients or staff members
💡 Better Alternatives
  • Instead of "Why didn't you take your meds?" → "Tell me about any challenges you've had with taking your medications"
  • Instead of "Don't worry, you'll be fine" → "It's understandable to feel worried. What concerns you most?"
  • Instead of giving advice → "What options are you considering? Let's talk through them together"
⚡ Disruptive Interpersonal Behavior +
📌 Types of Disruptive Behavior
  • Incivility: Rude, disrespectful behavior
  • Horizontal Violence: Between nurses at same level
  • Lateral Violence: Similar to horizontal
  • Nurse Bullying: Repeated aggressive behavior
  • Professional Incivility: Unprofessional conduct
✅ Nursing Response
  • Maintain professional demeanor
  • Respond assertively
  • Address issues directly with those involved
  • Seek guidance through nurse-managers
🚨 Verbally & Physically Aggressive Patients +
⚠️ Workplace Violence Definition

Workplace violence: Violent acts, including physical assaults and threats of assault, directed toward persons at work or on duty.

🗣️ Verbal Aggression

  • Harassing
  • Threatening
  • Yelling
  • Bullying
  • Hostile sarcasm
  • Name-calling
  • Profanity

👊 Physical Aggression

  • Hitting or slapping
  • Kicking
  • Biting
  • Pushing
  • Throwing objects
  • Grabbing
  • Spitting
✅ Nursing Response to Aggressive Patients
  • Maintain professional demeanor - stay calm, don't take it personally
  • Respond assertively - set clear boundaries without being aggressive
  • Address the issue directly - "I understand you're frustrated, but I can't allow you to speak to me that way"
  • Seek guidance and support through nurse-managers or appropriate channels
  • Ensure your safety - maintain distance, have an exit path, call for help if needed
  • Document the incident thoroughly
💡 De-escalation Techniques
  • Use a calm, low voice - don't match their volume or intensity
  • Maintain safe distance - stay at least arm's length away
  • Use open body language - don't cross arms or point fingers
  • Listen actively - sometimes patients just want to be heard
  • Acknowledge their feelings - "I can see you're very upset"
  • Offer choices - gives patient sense of control
  • Set limits clearly - "I want to help you, but I need you to lower your voice"
💡 Clinical Example

Scenario: Mr. Brown has been waiting 2 hours in the ED. He stands up, slams his fist on the counter, and yells, "This is ridiculous! I demand to see a doctor NOW or I'm going to sue everyone here!"

Therapeutic Response:

  • Remain calm, speak slowly and softly
  • "Mr. Brown, I can see you're very frustrated with the wait time." (acknowledge feelings)
  • "I understand this is difficult. Let me check on your status right now." (offer action)
  • "I want to help you, and I can do that better if we can talk calmly." (set limit kindly)
  • If he continues: "I'm going to step away and give you a moment. I'll be back in 5 minutes to check on you."
⚠️ When to Get Help Immediately
  • Patient has a weapon or is threatening with an object
  • Patient is physically attacking staff or other patients
  • Patient is destroying property
  • De-escalation is not working and situation is escalating
  • You feel unsafe at any point - trust your instincts!
📱 Electronic Communication & Social Media +
⚠️ Critical Reminders
  • ANA and NCSBN have guidelines for RNs using social media
  • Risk for HIPAA violations exists with ANY electronic message
  • Agencies have security measures in place
  • Includes: Social media, email, text messages, telehealth

Chapter 8 Wrap-Up: Communication

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Communication is a circular process: Sender → Message → Channel → Receiver → Feedback
  • Nonverbal communication (body language, facial expressions, touch) often speaks louder than words
  • The therapeutic relationship has 3 phases: Orientation, Working, Termination
  • SBAR provides a standardized way to communicate patient information clearly
  • Assertive communication respects both yourself AND others
  • Blocks to communication include false reassurance, giving advice, and changing the subject

🔑 Key Terms to Know

SBAR Therapeutic Relationship Intrapersonal Interpersonal Empathy Assertive vs Aggressive Open-ended Questions Validating Questions False Reassurance Horizontal Violence

💡 Remember for the Exam

Touch means different things to different people - it's NOT universal! The primary source of patient information is the PATIENT. Intrapersonal = self-talk (inside yourself). The therapeutic relationship is purposeful and time-limited, built on the patient's needs. Always respond to aggressive patients by maintaining professionalism and using de-escalation techniques.

📖 Chapter 9: Teaching and Counseling

Become an effective educator and counselor for your patients

🎯 Aims of Teaching and Counseling +

🧠 MNEMONIC: "MRFCP" - Aims of Teaching (Hover for examples!)

M
Maintaining Health
Maintaining Health
Teaching patients how to keep their current health status and prevent decline.
Teaching a healthy adult about proper nutrition, exercise, and sleep habits to maintain wellness.
R
Restoring Health
Restoring Health
Teaching patients how to recover from illness or injury and return to their baseline.
Teaching post-surgical patient about wound care, activity restrictions, and signs of infection to watch for.
F
Facilitating Coping
Facilitating Coping
Helping patients adjust to and manage chronic conditions or life changes they cannot fully reverse.
Teaching a newly diagnosed diabetic how to manage their condition, including emotional support for lifestyle adjustments.
C
Creating Outcomes
Creating Positive Outcomes
Establishing measurable goals and helping patients achieve optimal health results.
Setting a goal with a CHF patient to weigh themselves daily and call if they gain 3+ lbs, preventing hospital readmission.
P
Preventing Illness
Preventing Illness
Teaching patients how to avoid disease and injury through preventive measures.
Teaching about immunizations, cancer screenings, hand hygiene, safe sex practices, and fall prevention.
✅ Teaching Outcomes
  • Optimal wellness & self-care practices
  • Disease prevention/early detection
  • Quick recovery with minimal complications
  • Enhanced ability to adjust to life changes
📚 Key Teaching Concepts +

👂 Listen First

Listen to patients AND their families

Example: Before teaching, ask "What do you already know about your condition?"

⏰ Every Moment Counts

Every interaction is an opportunity to teach

Example: While giving medications, explain what each pill does and why it's important.

🎯 Patient-Centered

Keep education focused on the patient

Example: Ask "What matters most to you?" rather than listing everything YOU think is important.

🚀 Start Early

Begin teaching at the FIRST patient encounter

🎭 Factors Affecting Patient Learning +
📌 Hover over each letter to see examples!
These factors can enhance or hinder a patient's ability to learn.

🧠 MNEMONIC: "AFCCH" - Factors Affecting Learning

A
Age & Development
Age & Developmental Level
Learning ability and style change across the lifespan. Tailor teaching to cognitive and developmental stage.
Use play and simple words with children; use analogies and build on experience with adults; allow extra time and repeat information with elderly patients.
F
Family Support
Family/Caregiver Support
Support systems greatly impact learning success. Include caregivers in teaching when appropriate.
Teaching insulin administration to both the diabetic patient AND their spouse who helps manage their care at home.
C
Culture & Language
Cultural Influences & Language
Cultural beliefs affect health practices. Language barriers require interpreters or translated materials.
A patient's culture may view illness as spiritual punishment; some cultures defer health decisions to family elders. Always use a certified interpreter, not family members, for medical translation.
C
Cash (Financial)
Financial Resources
Economic factors affect ability to follow treatment plans. Consider cost when making recommendations.
A patient may not fill expensive prescriptions or buy special diet foods. Ask about financial concerns and connect with social work or patient assistance programs.
H
Health Literacy
Health Literacy
The ability to read, understand, and act on health information. Many adults have limited health literacy even if educated in other areas.
A patient may nod and say "yes" but not understand instructions. Use teach-back method: "Tell me in your own words how you'll take this medication."
💡 Assessing Readiness to Learn
  • Physical readiness: Is the patient in pain? Fatigued? Nauseated?
  • Emotional readiness: Is the patient anxious? In denial? Grieving?
  • Cognitive readiness: Is the patient alert? Oriented? On sedating medications?

Example: A patient who just received a cancer diagnosis is likely not ready to learn about chemotherapy side effects - address emotional needs first.

🎓 Knowles' Adult Learning Assumptions +
📌 Four Key Assumptions
Assumption Clinical Application
As people mature, self-concept moves from dependence → independence Let patient make choices: "Would you prefer to do your injection before or after breakfast?"
Previous experience is a rich resource for learning "Have you cared for anyone with diabetes before? What did you learn from that experience?"
Readiness to learn relates to developmental tasks or social roles New mom is ready to learn breastfeeding; newly retired patient ready to learn about managing chronic illness
Adults want material to be useful IMMEDIATELY Focus on "Here's what you need to know before going home TODAY" not "Someday you might need this"

💡 Test Tip!

Adults want to use what they learn RIGHT NOW - not "someday." This is a common test question!

Example: A patient just diagnosed with hypertension wants to know what to do NOW (take medication, reduce salt), not a detailed lecture on the pathophysiology of blood pressure.

💡 Applying Knowles in Practice
  • Involve the patient in planning their education
  • Build on what they already know
  • Make it relevant to their life right now
  • Respect their autonomy and experience
👴 Teaching Plans for Older Adults +
  • Identify learning barriers first
  • Allow extra time - don't rush!
  • Plan SHORT sessions - attention span
  • Accommodate sensory deficits (vision, hearing)
  • Reduce distractions in environment
  • Relate new info to familiar activities/information
🧠 Three Learning Domains +
⚠️ High-Yield for Exams!
Know these domains AND the teaching strategies for each!
Domain Focus Patient Example Best Strategies
Cognitive Knowledge & recall Patient explains why they need to limit sodium intake Lecture, written materials, discussion
Psychomotor Physical skills Patient demonstrates how to give themselves an insulin injection Demonstration, return demonstration, practice
Affective Attitudes, values, feelings Patient expresses willingness to quit smoking Role modeling, discussion, support groups
💡 More Clinical Examples
  • Cognitive: Patient correctly lists 3 warning signs of hypoglycemia; patient states when to call the doctor after surgery
  • Psychomotor: Patient correctly performs wound care; patient uses an inhaler with proper technique; patient walks with crutches safely
  • Affective: Patient verbalizes acceptance of diabetes diagnosis; patient shows motivation to attend cardiac rehab; patient expresses reduced anxiety about procedure

💡 Remember!

  • Cognitive = THINKING (brain knowledge) - they can EXPLAIN it
  • Psychomotor = DOING (physical skills) - they can DEMONSTRATE it
  • Affective = FEELING (emotions/attitudes) - they EXPRESS willingness/acceptance
📋 Assessment of the Learner +
📌 Before Teaching, Assess These!
Understanding your learner is essential for effective teaching.

🧠 MNEMONIC: "NRSMS" - Assessing the Learner (Hover for details!)

N
Needs
Learning Needs
What does the patient need to learn? What knowledge gaps exist? What skills must they acquire?

R
Readiness
Readiness to Learn

Learning Style

Motivation

  • Motivation: "What's most important to you about managing your health?"
  • Strengths: "Who helps you at home? What has worked for you before?"
✅ Promoting Patient Adherence +

Adherence is the preferred term over "compliance" because it:

🏆 Role of the Nurse as Coach +
📌 Coaching Steps
  1. Establish relationships & identify readiness for change
  2. Identify opportunities, issues, concerns
  3. Establish patient-centered goals
  4. Create structure of coaching interaction
  5. Empower and motivate patients to reach goals
  6. Assist patient to determine progress toward goals
💡 Coaching Example

Scenario: Mr. Lee, 55, needs to lose 30 lbs to manage his hypertension.

  • Step 1: Build rapport, assess if he's ready to make changes ("On a scale of 1-10, how ready are you?")
  • Step 2: Explore barriers ("What makes it hard to eat healthy?")
  • Step 3: Set HIS goals ("What's a realistic goal for you this week?")
  • Step 4: Plan regular check-ins (weekly weigh-ins, food diary review)
  • Step 5: Celebrate small wins ("You lost 2 lbs! How did that feel?")
  • Step 6: Review progress and adjust plan as needed

💭 Nurse as Counselor +
📌 Counseling Focus Areas
  • Improving coping abilities
  • Reinforcing healthy behaviors
  • Fostering positive interactions

⏱️ Short-term Counseling

For situational crisis (acute events)

Examples:
  • Patient just received cancer diagnosis
  • Family member died unexpectedly
  • Patient experienced a car accident
  • New amputation patient

📅 Long-term Counseling

For developmental crisis (life stage transitions)

Examples:
  • Adolescent struggling with identity
  • New parent adjusting to role
  • Empty nester depression
  • Retirement adjustment difficulties
💡 Counseling Example in Practice

Scenario: Mr. Peters, 45, just learned he needs a colostomy. He's tearful and says, "My life is over. I'll never be normal again."

Nurse's counseling response:

  • "This is a lot to process. It's completely normal to feel overwhelmed right now." (validates feelings)
  • "Tell me more about what concerns you most." (explores feelings)
  • "Would you like to speak with someone who has been living with a colostomy?" (offers resources)
  • Arrange follow-up with ostomy nurse and support group (long-term support)
✅ Key Skills

Warmth, friendliness, openness, empathy - CARING is fundamental!

🌍 Culturally Competent Patient Education +
📌 What is Cultural Competence?

The ability to provide care that respects and responds to patients' cultural backgrounds, beliefs, and values. It requires ongoing self-reflection and learning.

✅ Key Principles
  • Develop understanding of patient's culture
  • Work with multicultural team members
  • Be aware of YOUR OWN assumptions, biases, prejudices
  • Understand core cultural values of patient/group
  • Develop written materials in patient's preferred language
  • Use certified medical interpreters (NOT family members)

🌍 Cultural Considerations by Area:

Area Cultural Variations Nursing Response
Eye Contact Some cultures view direct eye contact as disrespectful (Asian, Native American, some Middle Eastern) Don't assume lack of eye contact means dishonesty or disinterest
Touch Some cultures restrict touch between opposite genders; some find head touching offensive Ask permission; offer same-gender caregiver when possible
Personal Space Varies widely - some cultures prefer closer proximity, others need more distance Observe patient's comfort and adjust accordingly
Decision Making Some cultures defer to family elders or male family members for health decisions Ask who should be included in care discussions
Health Beliefs May include traditional remedies, spiritual healing, "hot/cold" illness theory Ask about current treatments; integrate safe practices when possible
Diet Religious restrictions (halal, kosher, vegetarian), fasting practices Assess dietary needs; involve dietitian; time medications appropriately

💡 Culturally Competent Teaching Examples

  • Language: Use certified interpreter for Spanish-speaking diabetic patient; provide written materials in Spanish
  • Family: Include patient's adult son in teaching when patient defers to him for decisions
  • Beliefs: Acknowledge patient's use of herbal teas while teaching about medication interactions
  • Diet: Teach Muslim patient about managing diabetes during Ramadan fasting

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using family members (especially children) as interpreters for medical information
  • Assuming all members of a culture share the same beliefs
  • Dismissing traditional practices without understanding them
  • Imposing your own cultural values on the patient
  • Assuming a patient who nods understands (may be politeness)
💡 Clinical Scenario Example

Patient: Mrs. Nguyen, 70-year-old Vietnamese woman with new diabetes diagnosis. Her adult daughter is present.

Culturally Competent Approach:

  • Use professional Vietnamese interpreter (not the daughter) for complex medical teaching
  • Ask Mrs. Nguyen who she wants involved in her care decisions
  • Inquire about traditional remedies she may be using
  • Discuss Vietnamese foods and how to adapt favorite recipes for diabetic diet
  • Provide written materials in Vietnamese
  • Use teach-back: "Can you tell me in your own words how you will take this medication?"
  • Respect that she may defer to her daughter or consult with family before making decisions

💡 Remember: ASK, Don't ASSUME!

"I want to make sure I provide the best care for you. Can you tell me about any cultural or religious practices that are important for me to know about?"

🎯

Chapter 9 Wrap-Up: Teaching & Counseling

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Teaching aims: Maintaining health, Restoring health, Facilitating coping, Creating outcomes, Preventing illness
  • Three learning domains: Cognitive (thinking), Psychomotor (doing), Affective (feeling)
  • Adults want information that is useful IMMEDIATELY - not "someday" (Knowles' theory)
  • Psychomotor skills are best taught through demonstration + return demonstration
  • Adherence = patient actively participates in care decisions; Compliance = simply following orders
  • Short-term counseling for situational crises; Long-term counseling for developmental crises

🔑 Key Terms to Know

Cognitive Domain Psychomotor Domain Affective Domain Knowles' Theory Adherence vs Compliance Situational Crisis Developmental Crisis Health Literacy Teach-Back Method Cultural Competence

💡 Remember for the Exam

Demonstration + Return Demonstration is the BEST strategy for teaching psychomotor skills (like insulin injection or wound care). Adults want to use information NOW - they're not interested in learning things "just in case." Developmental crises (adolescence, retirement, empty nest) need long-term counseling, while situational crises (accident, diagnosis, death) need short-term support. Always assess health literacy before teaching!

🩺 Chapter 27: Health Assessment

Master physical assessment techniques and health history collection

📋 Health Assessment Overview +
📌 Definition
Health assessment involves collecting, validating, and analyzing data about a patient's health status.

💭 Subjective Data

Based on patient experiences and perceptions

Example: "I feel dizzy"

📏 Objective Data

Measurable and directly observed

Example: BP 140/90, Temp 101°F

✅ Two Main Components
  • Health History: Collection of SUBJECTIVE information
  • Physical Assessment: Collection of OBJECTIVE data

💡 Test Tip!

Subjective = what the patient SAYS (symptoms)
Objective = what you can SEE/MEASURE (signs)

📝 Health History Assessment Process +
📌 What is a Health History?

A comprehensive collection of subjective data obtained through interviewing the patient. It provides the foundation for understanding the patient's health status, concerns, and needs.

⚠️ Primary Source

The PATIENT is always the primary source of health history data. Secondary sources (family, medical records, other providers) supplement but don't replace patient information.

📋 Steps of the Health History Interview:

Step What to Do Example Questions/Actions
1. Preparation Review chart, ensure privacy, gather forms Close curtain, sit at eye level, have pen and clipboard ready
2. Introduction Introduce yourself, explain purpose, establish rapport "Hi, I'm Sarah, your nurse. I'm going to ask you some questions about your health to help us provide the best care."
3. Biographical Data Collect identifying information Name, DOB, address, emergency contact, insurance, occupation
4. Chief Complaint Ask why they're here TODAY in their own words "What brought you to the hospital today?" Document in patient's exact words.
5. History of Present Illness Get details about the current problem Use OLDCARTS: Onset, Location, Duration, Character, Aggravating factors, Relieving factors, Timing, Severity
6. Past Health History Previous illnesses, surgeries, hospitalizations "Have you ever been hospitalized before? Any surgeries? Chronic conditions?"
7. Medications & Allergies Current meds, OTC, supplements, allergies "What medications do you take? Any allergies to medications, foods, or latex?"
8. Family History Health conditions that run in family "Do any diseases run in your family? Parents, siblings, grandparents?"
9. Social History Lifestyle, habits, living situation Tobacco, alcohol, drugs, occupation, exercise, diet, living situation, support system
10. Review of Systems Systematic questions about each body system "Any headaches? Vision changes? Chest pain? Shortness of breath?" etc.

🔬 Physical Assessment Process +
📌 What is Physical Assessment?

A systematic collection of objective data through examination of the patient's body systems using inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation.

📋 Steps of Physical Assessment:

Step Actions Tips
1. Preparation Gather equipment, wash hands, prepare environment Warm stethoscope, ensure adequate lighting, comfortable room temp
2. Patient Prep Explain procedure, provide gown, ensure privacy "I'm going to examine you now. It shouldn't hurt. Let me know if you're uncomfortable."
3. General Survey Overall impression before touching patient Appearance, posture, mobility, speech, mental status, vital signs, height/weight
4. Head-to-Toe Exam Systematic examination of each body system Work top to bottom OR by body system; use consistent approach
5. Documentation Record findings accurately and completely Document normal AND abnormal findings; use objective terms

🔍 Head-to-Toe Assessment Order:

👤 Head & Neck

  • Inspect head shape, symmetry, hair
  • Eyes: PERRLA (see below!)
  • Ears: hearing, external appearance
  • Nose & sinuses: patency, discharge
  • Mouth & throat: mucosa, teeth, tonsils
  • Neck: ROM, lymph nodes, thyroid, JVD

🫁 Chest & Lungs

  • Inspect: chest shape, symmetry, breathing pattern
  • Palpate: chest expansion, fremitus
  • Percuss: resonance vs dullness
  • Auscultate: breath sounds all lobes
  • Note: adventitious sounds (crackles, wheezes)

❤️ Cardiovascular

  • Inspect: JVD, skin color, edema
  • Palpate: PMI, pulses (radial, pedal, etc.)
  • Auscultate: heart sounds S1, S2, murmurs
  • Note: capillary refill, peripheral edema

🔴 Abdomen

  • ORDER: I-A-P-P (Auscultate BEFORE touching!)
  • Inspect: contour, symmetry, scars
  • Auscultate: bowel sounds all 4 quadrants
  • Percuss: tympany vs dullness
  • Palpate: light then deep, tenderness, masses

💡 How to Perform PERRLA Assessment
Step Action What You're Looking For
1. Inspect Look at both pupils in normal lighting Size (2-6mm normal), shape (round), equality (same size bilaterally)
2. Direct Light Dim room, shine penlight into RIGHT eye RIGHT pupil should constrict briskly
3. Consensual While light in RIGHT eye, watch LEFT eye LEFT pupil should also constrict (consensual response)
4. Repeat Shine penlight into LEFT eye LEFT constricts (direct), RIGHT constricts (consensual)
5. Accommodation Have patient look far, then at your finger close up Pupils constrict when focusing on near object

⚠️ Abnormal Findings

  • Unequal pupils: May indicate stroke, brain injury, increased ICP
  • Fixed & dilated: Severe brain injury, drug overdose (stimulants)
  • Pinpoint pupils: Opioid overdose, pontine hemorrhage
  • Sluggish reaction: Increased ICP, CN III damage
  • Non-reactive: Neurological emergency - notify provider STAT!

📝 Documentation Example

  • Normal: "PERRLA, pupils 3mm bilaterally, brisk reaction"
  • Abnormal: "Right pupil 5mm, left pupil 3mm, right sluggish to light, left brisk. Accommodation intact bilaterally."
⚠️ Critical Points
  • New unequal pupils + altered LOC = EMERGENCY - could indicate herniation
  • Some people have naturally unequal pupils (physiologic anisocoria) - check baseline!
  • Certain eye drops (dilating drops) will affect pupil reactivity - ask about recent eye exams
  • Cataracts or artificial lenses may affect appearance but pupils should still react

💪 Musculoskeletal

  • Inspect: posture, gait, symmetry
  • Palpate: muscle tone, tenderness
  • Assess: ROM, strength (grip, push/pull)
  • Compare bilateral sides

🧠 Neurological

  • Mental status: LOC, orientation (A&O x3)
  • Cranial nerves (if indicated)
  • Motor: strength, coordination
  • Sensory: touch, pain, temperature
  • Reflexes (if indicated)

🧴 Integumentary

  • Inspect: color, lesions, rashes, wounds
  • Palpate: temperature, turgor, moisture
  • Note: edema, bruising, pressure areas
  • Check nail beds, hair distribution
💡 Documentation Example

Good documentation: "Lungs clear to auscultation bilaterally. Respirations 18, unlabored. No adventitious sounds noted. Chest expansion symmetric."

Poor documentation: "Lungs normal." (Too vague - doesn't describe what was assessed)

⚠️ Always Document
  • What you assessed
  • What you found (normal AND abnormal)
  • Any patient complaints during assessment
  • Comparison to previous assessments if applicable

I
Inspection
Inspection

Technique Method What It Assesses Clinical Example
Inspection Deliberate, purposeful observation Size, color, shape, position, symmetry Observing skin for rashes, watching chest rise and fall, noting facial expressions

⚠️ EXCEPTION for Abdomen!

For abdominal assessment, the order is: Inspection → Auscultation → Percussion → Palpation

(Auscultate BEFORE percussion/palpation to avoid altering bowel sounds!)

Why? Touching, pressing, or tapping the abdomen can stimulate or slow bowel activity, giving you inaccurate bowel sound assessment.

💡 Palpation Examples

  • Skin turgor: Pinch skin on forearm - does it snap back quickly (good) or stay tented (dehydration)?
  • Temperature: Use back of hand to feel if skin is warm, hot, or cool
  • Vibrations: Feel for fremitus (vibrations when patient says "99")
  • Masses: Palpate abdomen for any lumps or tenderness

Wheezes = musical, squeaking, HIGH-PITCHED sound (narrowed airways)

🔊 Sound Characteristics (Auscultation):

🧠 "PLQD" - Sound Characteristics (Hover for examples!)

P
Pitch
Pitch
How high or low the sound is. Ranges from high-pitched to low-pitched.
High-pitched: Wheezes, S1/S2 heart sounds. Low-pitched: S3/S4, rhonchi, normal bowel sounds.
L
Loudness
Loudness
How loud or soft the sound is. Can indicate severity or location.
Loud murmur may indicate severe valve problem. Decreased/absent breath sounds may indicate pneumothorax or effusion.
Q
Quality
Quality
The character or description of the sound - what does it sound like?
Musical (wheezes), gurgling (bowel sounds), swishing (murmur), crackling (crackles), grating (friction rub).
D
Duration
Duration
How long the sound lasts - short, medium, or long/continuous.
S1/S2 are short sounds. Murmurs may be continuous. Crackles are brief; wheezes are continuous.

👶 Infant/Children Variations

  • Visible cardiac pulsation (thin chest)
  • Sinus dysrhythmia (normal! - rate varies with breathing)
  • S3 present in 1/3 of children (normal)
  • More rapid heart rate until age 8

👴 Older Adult Variations

  • Difficult-to-palpate apical pulse
  • Difficult-to-palpate distal arteries
  • Prominent, tortuous vessels (varicosities)
  • Increased systolic & diastolic BP
  • Widening pulse pressure

💡 Orientation Assessment

Level of Awareness: Orientation to Time, Place, Person

  • Person: "What is your name?" "Do you know who I am?"
  • Place: "Where are you right now?" "What hospital is this?"
  • Time: "What day is it?" "What month/year is it?"

Documentation example: "Patient is A&O x3" (alert and oriented to person, place, and time)

📌 Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

Standardized tool measuring: Eye opening, Verbal response, Motor response

Score range: 3-15 (lower = more severe impairment)

  • 15: Fully alert and oriented
  • 13-14: Minor impairment
  • 9-12: Moderate impairment
  • 3-8: Severe impairment / coma

Chapter 27 Wrap-Up: Health Assessment

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Subjective data = what the patient SAYS (symptoms); Objective data = what you can MEASURE (signs)
  • Four types of assessment: Comprehensive, Ongoing, Focused, Emergency (COFE)
  • Assessment techniques in order: Inspection → Palpation → Percussion → Auscultation (IPPA)
  • EXCEPTION: Abdominal assessment = Inspection → Auscultation → Percussion → Palpation
  • Sims' position is used for rectal and vaginal exams
  • Palpation assesses turgor (skin elasticity) - important for dehydration assessment
  • Wheezes are high-pitched, musical sounds indicating narrowed airways

🔑 Key Terms to Know

Subjective vs Objective IPPA Inspection Palpation Percussion Auscultation Sims' Position Turgor Erythema Cyanosis Wheezes Crackles Glasgow Coma Scale

🩺 Integumentary Terms Quick Review

  • Erythema = Redness
  • Cyanosis = Blue/gray color (low oxygen)
  • Jaundice = Yellow color (liver issues)
  • Pallor = Paleness (anemia)
  • Ecchymosis = Bruise
  • Petechiae = Tiny red dots (capillary bleeding)
  • Diaphoresis = Excessive sweating
  • Edema = Swelling from fluid

💡 Remember for the Exam

For abdominal assessment, ALWAYS auscultate BEFORE palpating or percussing - touching can alter bowel sounds! The primary source of health history data is the PATIENT, not family. Sims' position (side-lying) = rectal/vaginal exams. Wheezes are musical and high-pitched; Rhonchi are low-pitched and snoring. GCS scores range from 3-15 (lower = worse).
