💬 Chapter 8: Communication

Master the art of therapeutic communication in nursing practice

🔄 The Communication Process (Berlo's Model) +
📌 Remember This
Communication is a dynamic, circular process - not just one-way!

🧠 MNEMONIC: "SMCRF" - Some Messages Create Real Feedback

SStimulus
MMessage
CChannel
RReceiver
FFeedback
  • Stimulus: Initiates the communication
  • Sender/Encoder: Person who begins the message
  • Message: The communication product
  • Channel: Auditory, visual, or kinesthetic pathway
  • Receiver/Decoder: Translates and interprets the message
  • Feedback: Confirms the message was received
🗣️ Forms of Communication +

💬 Verbal Communication

Depends on understanding of language between sender and receiver

🤫 Nonverbal Communication

  • Facial expressions & eye contact
  • Gestures & touch
  • Body movements, posture, gait
  • Space & territoriality
  • Physical appearance & dress
  • Sounds (moaning, crying, sighing)
⚠️ Test Alert
Touch is a personal behavior that means different things to different people - it's NOT universal!
📊 Four Levels of Communication +
Level Description Example
Intrapersonal Self-talk; communication within a person "I've got this" before a presentation
Interpersonal Between two or more people to exchange messages Nurse talking with patient
Small Group Communication within a small team Nursing team huddle
Organizational Communication across an organization Hospital-wide memos

💡 Quick Tip

Intrapersonal = Inside yourself (think: intra-venous goes INSIDE the vein)

📋 SBAR Communication Technique +
📌 Definition
SBAR is a standardized hand-off communication technique used to relay patient information clearly and concisely.

S - Situation

What is happening with the patient RIGHT NOW?

B - Background

What is the clinical background/context?

A - Assessment

What do I think the problem is?

R - Recommendation

What do I think should be done?

✅ Patient Safety Goal
SBAR supports Joint Commission National Patient Safety Goals for improving communication!
🤝 Therapeutic Nurse-Patient Relationship +
📌 Key Characteristics
  • Does NOT occur spontaneously - requires intention
  • Characterized by unequal sharing (patient shares more)
  • Built on the PATIENT'S needs
  • Nurse is professionally accountable
  • Purposeful and TIME-LIMITED

Three Phases of the Therapeutic Relationship:

Phase What Happens Key Activity
1. Orientation Establishing the relationship Contract/agreement made about goals, frequency, duration
2. Working Achieving goals Nurse provides assistance, counseling, teaching
3. Termination Ending the relationship Examine goals for attainment

😊 Dispositional Traits

  • Warmth & friendliness
  • Openness & respect
  • Empathy
  • Honesty & authenticity
  • Trust & caring
  • Competence

🔨 Rapport Builders

  • Specific objectives
  • Comfortable environment
  • Privacy
  • Confidentiality
  • Patient (not task) focus

❓ Interviewing Techniques +
Technique Purpose
Open-ended Allows patient to express freely; cannot be answered yes/no
Closed Specific, limited response expected
Validating Confirms what nurse heard or observed
Clarifying Makes unclear statements more understandable
Reflective Encourages deeper exploration
Sequencing Establishes order of events

⚠️ Test Tip
Validating questions confirm what nurse heard - NOT open-ended questions!
💪 Assertive vs Aggressive Behavior +

✅ Assertive (GOOD)

  • Confident, open body posture
  • Clear, concise "I" statements
  • Shares thoughts effectively
  • Works with or without supervision
  • Remains calm under supervision
  • Asks for help when needed
  • Admits mistakes, takes responsibility

❌ Aggressive (BAD)

  • Violates rights of others
  • Can be verbal or physical
  • Marked by tension and anger
  • Angry tone, condescending
  • Threatening behavior
  • Focus on "winning at all costs"
  • Inhibits collaboration
🚫 Blocks to Communication +
⚠️ Avoid These!
  • Failure to perceive patient as human being
  • Failure to listen
  • Using clichés
  • Using "why" and "how" questions (can feel accusatory)
  • Probing for information
  • Using leading questions
  • Giving advice
  • Making judgmental comments
  • Changing the subject
  • Giving false assurance
  • Using gossip and rumors
⚡ Disruptive Interpersonal Behavior +
📌 Types of Disruptive Behavior
  • Incivility: Rude, disrespectful behavior
  • Horizontal Violence: Between nurses at same level
  • Lateral Violence: Similar to horizontal
  • Nurse Bullying: Repeated aggressive behavior
  • Professional Incivility: Unprofessional conduct
✅ Nursing Response
  • Maintain professional demeanor
  • Respond assertively
  • Address issues directly with those involved
  • Seek guidance through nurse-managers
📱 Electronic Communication & Social Media +
⚠️ Critical Reminders
  • ANA and NCSBN have guidelines for RNs using social media
  • Risk for HIPAA violations exists with ANY electronic message
  • Agencies have security measures in place
  • Includes: Social media, email, text messages, telehealth

Become an effective educator and counselor for your patients

MMaintaining health
RRestoring health
FFacilitating coping
CCreating outcomes

✅ Teaching Outcomes
  • Optimal wellness & self-care practices

📚 Key Teaching Concepts +

👂 Listen First

Listen to patients AND their families

⏰ Every Moment Counts

Every interaction is an opportunity to teach

🎯 Patient-Centered

Keep education focused on the patient

🚀 Start Early

Begin teaching at the FIRST patient encounter

🎭 Factors Affecting Patient Learning +

🧠 MNEMONIC: "A FCCH" - A Few Caring Concepts Help

AAge & developmental level
FFamily/caregiver support
CCultural influences & Language
CCash (Financial resources)
HHealth literacy
🎓 Knowles' Adult Learning Assumptions +
📌 Four Key Assumptions
  1. As people mature, self-concept moves from dependence → independence
  2. Previous experience is a rich resource for learning
  3. Readiness to learn relates to developmental tasks or social roles
  4. Adults want material to be useful IMMEDIATELY (not in the future)

💡 Test Tip!

Adults want to use what they learn RIGHT NOW - not "someday." This is a common test question!

👴 Teaching Plans for Older Adults +
  • Identify learning barriers first
  • Allow extra time - don't rush!
  • Plan SHORT sessions - attention span
  • Accommodate sensory deficits (vision, hearing)
  • Reduce distractions in environment
  • Relate new info to familiar activities/information
🧠 Three Learning Domains +
⚠️ High-Yield for Exams!
Know these domains AND the teaching strategies for each!
Domain Focus Example Best Strategies
Cognitive Knowledge & recall Patient describes calorie counting Lecture, panel, discovery, written materials
Psychomotor Physical skills Patient demonstrates wound care Demonstration, discovery, printed materials
Affective Attitudes, values, feelings Patient expresses renewed confidence Role modeling, discussion, audiovisual

💡 Remember!

  • Cognitive = thinking (brain knowledge)
  • Psychomotor = doing (physical skills)
  • Affective = feeling (emotions/attitudes)

✅ Promoting Patient Adherence +
📌 Adherence vs Compliance

Adherence is the preferred term over "compliance" because it:

  • Uses a TEAM approach
  • Reflects patient's RIGHT to choose
  • Supports active patient participation
✅ How to Promote Adherence
  • Clear instructions that support patient goals
  • Include patient/caregivers as PARTNERS
  • Use interactive teaching strategies
  • Develop strong interpersonal relationships
📝 Teaching Strategies & Methods +

📚 Materials

  • Audiovisual materials
  • Printed materials
  • Programmed instruction
  • Web-based/technology

🎯 Methods

  • Lecture
  • Discussion
  • Panel discussion
  • Demonstration
  • Discovery
  • Role playing
⚠️ Test Alert
Demonstration is the best strategy for psychomotor learning (like catheter care!)
🏆 Role of the Nurse as Coach +
  1. Establish relationships & identify readiness for change
  2. Identify opportunities, issues, concerns
  3. Establish patient-centered goals
  4. Create structure of coaching interaction
  5. Empower and motivate patients to reach goals
  6. Assist patient to determine progress toward goals
📄 Documentation of Teaching-Learning +
📌 What to Document
  • Summary of the learning need
  • The plan developed
  • Implementation of the plan
  • Evaluation results
💭 Nurse as Counselor +
📌 Counseling Focus Areas
  • Improving coping abilities
  • Reinforcing healthy behaviors
  • Fostering positive interactions
  • Preventing illness and disability

⏱️ Short-term Counseling

For situational crisis (acute events)

📅 Long-term Counseling

For developmental crisis (life stage transitions)

✅ Key Skills

Warmth, friendliness, openness, empathy - CARING is fundamental!

🌍 Culturally Competent Patient Education +
  • Develop understanding of patient's culture
  • Work with multicultural team
  • Be aware of personal assumptions, biases, prejudices
  • Understand core cultural values of patient/group
  • Develop written material in patient's preferred language

🩺 Chapter 27: Health Assessment

Master physical assessment techniques and health history collection

📋 Health Assessment Overview +
📌 Definition
Health assessment involves collecting, validating, and analyzing data about a patient's health status.

💭 Subjective Data

Based on patient experiences and perceptions

Example: "I feel dizzy"

📏 Objective Data

Measurable and directly observed

Example: BP 140/90, Temp 101°F

✅ Two Main Components
  • Health History: Collection of SUBJECTIVE information
  • Physical Assessment: Collection of OBJECTIVE data

💡 Test Tip!

Subjective = what the patient SAYS (symptoms)
Objective = what you can SEE/MEASURE (signs)

📊 Types of Health Assessments +
Type When Used Scope
Comprehensive Upon admission to facility Complete head-to-toe
Ongoing/Partial At regular intervals Focused on systems of concern
Focused To assess specific problem One body system or concern
Emergency Life-threatening situations ABCs and vital functions

🏥 Preparing Patient & Environment +

👤 Patient Preparation

  • Consider physiologic & psychological needs
  • Explain the process thoroughly
  • Explain it should NOT be painful
  • Maintain privacy with drapes
  • Answer questions honestly

🏠 Environment Preparation

  • Ensure patient is pain-free as possible
  • Prepare examination table
  • Provide gown and drape
  • Gather supplies/instruments
  • Comfortable room temperature
  • Curtain/screen for privacy
✅ Assessment Considerations
  • Lifespan considerations
  • Cultural considerations and sensitivity
  • Patient preparation
  • Environmental preparations
🔍 Four Assessment Techniques (IPPA) +

🧠 MNEMONIC: "IPPA" - Assessment Techniques in Order

IInspection
PPalpation
PPercussion
AAuscultation
Technique Method What It Assesses
Inspection Deliberate, purposeful observation Size, color, shape, position, symmetry
Palpation Using sense of TOUCH Temperature, turgor, texture, moisture, vibrations, shape
Percussion Striking one object against another Location, shape, size, density of tissues
Auscultation Listening with stethoscope Pitch, loudness, quality, duration of sounds
⚠️ EXCEPTION for Abdomen!

For abdominal assessment, the order is: Inspection → Auscultation → Percussion → Palpation

(Auscultate BEFORE percussion/palpation to avoid altering bowel sounds!)

💡 Test Tip!

Palpation assesses TURGOR (skin elasticity) - this is commonly tested!

🛏️ Patient Positions for Assessment +
Position Used to Assess
Standing Posture, balance, gait
Sitting Upper body visualization
Supine Relaxation of abdominal muscles; head, neck, thorax, heart, breasts, abdomen, extremities
Dorsal Recumbent Same as supine; for patients having difficulty maintaining supine
Sims' (Side-lying) Rectum or vagina
Prone Hip joint and posterior thorax
Lithotomy Female genitalia and rectum
Knee-Chest Anus and rectum
⚠️ High-Yield!
Sims' position is used for rectal and vaginal assessment - commonly tested!
🧴 Integumentary Assessment (Skin) +
📌 Risk Factors to Identify
  • History of rashes, lesions, bruising, allergies
  • Exposure to sun, chemicals
  • Piercings or tattoos
  • Degree of mobility
  • Nutritional status

📚 Key Terminology:

Term Definition
Erythema Redness
Ecchymosis Collection of blood in subcutaneous tissue (bruise)

Cyanosis Bluish or grayish color
Jaundice Yellow color
Pallor Paleness
Diaphoresis Excessive perspiration (sweating)
Turgor Elasticity of skin

🫁 Thorax and Lung Assessment +
📌 Risk Factors to Identify
  • History of trauma or lung surgery
  • Number of pillows used when sleeping (orthopnea)
  • Cough, chest pain, allergies
  • Exposure to chemicals or smoke

🔊 Lung Sounds:

✅ Normal Sounds

  • Bronchial/Tubular: Over trachea
  • Bronchovesicular: Over main bronchi
  • Vesicular: Over lung periphery

⚠️ Adventitious (Abnormal)

  • Wheezes: Musical, high-pitched, squeaking
  • Rhonchi: Low-pitched, snoring, rumbling
  • Crackles: Popping, bubbling (like hair rubbing)
  • Stridor: High-pitched, crowing (airway obstruction)
  • Friction Rub: Grating sound
⚠️ Test Alert!
Wheezes = musical, squeaking, HIGH-PITCHED sound (narrowed airways)
✅ Older Adult Variations
  • Increased anteroposterior chest diameter
  • Increased dorsal spinal curve (kyphosis)
  • Decreased thoracic expansion
  • Use of accessory muscles to exhale
❤️ Cardiovascular & Peripheral Vascular Assessment +
📌 Risk Factors to Identify
  • History of chest pain, palpitations, dizziness
  • Swelling in ankles or feet
  • Medications
  • Personal or family history of heart disease
  • Type and amount of exercise
✅ What to Assess
  • Carotid arteries
  • Heart sounds
  • Peripheral pulses
  • Neurovascular status

🔊 Sound Characteristics (Auscultation):

🧠 "PLQD" - Sound Characteristics

PPitch
High to low
LLoudness
Soft to loud
QQuality
Gurgling, swishing
DDuration
Short, medium, long

👶 Infant/Children Variations

  • Visible cardiac pulsation (thin chest)
  • Sinus dysrhythmia (normal!)
  • S3 present in 1/3 of children
  • More rapid heart rate until age 8

👴 Older Adult Variations

  • Difficult-to-palpate apical pulse
  • Difficult-to-palpate distal arteries
  • Prominent, tortuous vessels
  • Increased BP
  • Widening pulse pressure
🔴 Abdominal Assessment +
⚠️ Critical Order!

Inspection → Auscultation → Percussion → Palpation

Auscultate BEFORE touching to avoid altering bowel sounds!

📌 Risk Factors to Identify
  • Abdominal pain, indigestion, nausea
  • Changes in bowel habits
  • Appetite changes
  • Alcohol ingestion
  • Menstrual history

📍 Four Quadrants:

RUQ (Right Upper)

Liver, gallbladder, duodenum, right kidney

LUQ (Left Upper)

Stomach, spleen, left kidney, pancreas

RLQ (Right Lower)

Appendix, right ovary, ascending colon

LLQ (Left Lower)

Left ovary, descending colon, sigmoid

🔍 Characteristics of Masses (Palpation):

  • Shape
  • Size
  • Consistency
  • Surface
  • Mobility
  • Tenderness
✅ Age Variations
  • Newborns: Umbilical cord present; dries and falls off in first few weeks
  • Children < 5: "Pot-belly" is normal; visible peristaltic waves
  • Older Adults: Decreased bowel sounds, decreased tone, fat accumulation
🧠 Neurological Assessment +
📌 Risk Factors to Identify
  • History of numbness, tingling, seizures, trembling
  • Headaches or dizziness
  • Trauma to head or spine
  • History of HTN or stroke
  • Changes in vision, hearing, taste, smell
  • History of diabetes or cardiovascular disease
  • Alcohol and medication use

🔍 Components of Neuro Assessment:

  • Mental status
  • Memory
  • Emotional status
  • Cognitive abilities and behavior
  • Cerebellar function (motor, coordination, balance)
  • Cranial nerve function
  • Motor and sensory function
  • Reflexes

🧠 Level of Consciousness:

Level Description
Awake/Alert Fully conscious, responsive
Lethargic Drowsy, easily aroused
Stuporous Difficult to arouse, confused when awakened
Comatose Cannot be aroused, unresponsive
✅ Level of Awareness

Orientation to: Time, Place, Person

📌 Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

Standardized tool measuring: Eye opening, Verbal response, Motor response

Score range: 3-15 (lower = more severe impairment)
